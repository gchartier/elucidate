# Question Type Library

Reference for question selection during elucidate inquiry. Organized by purpose, not by phase — most question types are useful across multiple phases. Each section includes example questions and guidance on when to use them.

## Orienting Questions

Open-ended, situation-mapping. Primary use in Phase 0, but useful whenever the inquiry shifts terrain or encounters a new domain.

**Purpose:** Establish the landscape. Understand what exists before trying to change or interpret it.

Examples:
- "What brought this to mind right now?" — surfaces motivation, recency, and emotional charge
- "What do you already know about this?" — establishes baseline, prevents re-teaching what the user already understands
- "What have you tried or considered so far?" — reveals prior work, dead ends, and implicit assumptions about what's relevant
- "Who or what is this for?" — grounds the inquiry in a use context; abstract understanding without use is rarely satisfying
- "What would it look like if you had this figured out?" — elicits success criteria and reveals what "understanding" means to this user for this topic
- "What's the most confusing part right now?" — finds the real entry point, which is almost never where the user started talking
- "If you had to explain this to someone right now, where would you get stuck?" — pinpoints the exact boundary between what they know and what they don't
- "What's your gut sense, even if you can't justify it yet?" — permits intuitive knowledge to enter the conversation

**When to stop orienting:** When you can articulate the situation-gap-use triad — what's the situation, where's the gap, what's the use. You don't need to ask all these questions. Some answers are obvious from context.

## Framing Questions

Assumption-surfacing, reframing probes, stasis diagnosis. Primary use in Phase 1, but useful whenever a frame starts failing or shifting.

**Purpose:** Find the shape of the problem. The right frame makes the right questions obvious; the wrong frame makes every question feel slightly off.

Examples:
- "What are you taking for granted here?" — the most important framing question; assumptions you don't examine control your thinking
- "Is this a question of what's true, what it means, what's good, or what to do?" — stasis diagnosis; each type requires different evidence and reasoning
- "What would someone who disagrees with you say?" — introduces an opposing frame without endorsing it
- "You said X — what do you mean by [ambiguous term]?" — definitional precision; most disagreements are definitional
- "Is this one question or several?" — decomposition probe; compound questions resist resolution
- "What would change your mind about this?" — reveals evidential commitments and what the user considers valid evidence
- "Here are three ways I could frame what you're asking: [A], [B], [C]. Which is closest?" — frame selection; give the user options and let them refine
- "When you say you want to 'understand' X, what would understanding look like? What could you do that you can't do now?" — operationalizes the goal
- "Is there something you're afraid might be true?" — surfaces anxious frames that distort inquiry
- "What's the simplest version of this question?" — strips away complexity to find the core

**When framing fails:** If no frame feels right after 3-4 attempts, the resistance is diagnostic. The user may be asking about something that doesn't fit existing categories, or there may be an emotional dimension that isn't being addressed. Consider asking a meta-question: "We've tried a few framings and none of them feel right. What do you think is making this hard to pin down?"

## Discriminating Questions

Questions where competing interpretations predict different answers. Primary use in Phase 2-3, when hypotheses need testing.

**Purpose:** Separate hypotheses that look similar from a distance but differ in specific predictions. The best discriminating question is one where answer X supports interpretation A and answer Y supports interpretation B.

Examples:
- "If [interpretation A] is right, then [prediction] should follow. Does it?" — direct hypothesis testing
- "Does this apply in [edge case]?" — boundary testing; edge cases discriminate between similar models
- "What's the difference between [concept X] and [near-neighbor concept Y]?" — distinction drawing; if the user can't articulate the difference, the concepts may be confused
- "You could explain this as [A] or as [B]. Which fits better, and why?" — forced comparison requiring articulated reasoning
- "If you had to bet, which explanation would you put money on?" — commitment elicitation; betting forces engagement with credence
- "What evidence would distinguish between these two possibilities?" — evidential planning; what would we need to know?
- "Imagine someone who agreed with your conclusion but for completely different reasons. What reasons might they give?" — separates conclusion from reasoning
- "Here's a scenario where interpretation A applies but interpretation B doesn't: [scenario]. Does this feel like a real case or a contrived one?" — ecological validity of discriminating scenarios
- "What's one thing that's definitely true, regardless of which interpretation is right?" — anchoring; finds common ground between hypotheses

**Selection principle:** The best discriminating question is one where you genuinely don't know what the user will say. If you can predict the answer, the question doesn't discriminate — it confirms. Seek surprise.

## Deepening Questions

Explanation requests, counterexample probes, evidence challenges. Primary use in Phase 3, when the frame is set and the investigation is underway.

**Purpose:** Turn assertions into understanding. An assertion is a claim. Understanding is a claim plus its justification, boundaries, mechanisms, and connections to other claims.

Examples:
- "Why do you think that is?" — the most basic deepening question; asks for explanation
- "Can you give me a specific example?" — concretization; abstractions without examples may be empty
- "Can you give me a counterexample — a case where this doesn't hold?" — boundary finding; a concept without a boundary is a slogan
- "What's the mechanism? How does X actually lead to Y?" — causal probing; correlation and causation need separating
- "How confident are you in that? What would shake your confidence?" — calibration; helps separate strong beliefs from weak ones
- "What's the strongest version of this claim? What's the weakest?" — steelmanning and strawmanning; tests the range of defensible positions
- "You said X causes Y. Could it be the reverse? Could Y cause X?" — causal direction challenge
- "What's missing from this picture?" — completeness check; what have we not discussed that matters?
- "Where did you learn this? How do you know?" — source and evidence tracing; beliefs from experience differ from beliefs from authority
- "You seem very certain about that. What if you're wrong?" — certainty probe; productive discomfort
- "How is this different from [common misunderstanding of the same topic]?" — tests whether the user's understanding goes beyond surface patterns
- "If a smart skeptic challenged you on this, what would their best argument be?" — adversarial testing from within

**Selection principle:** Deepening questions are most valuable when the user states something confidently but the basis isn't clear. Don't deepen claims that are offered tentatively — the user already knows they're uncertain. Deepen the things they're sure about.

## Verification Questions

Synthesis prompts, application tests, robustness checks. Primary use in Phase 4, when the understanding is substantially formed and needs testing.

**Purpose:** Determine whether the understanding is genuine and durable, or whether it's a comfortable summary that won't survive contact with reality.

Examples:
- "Here's my summary of what we've established: [summary]. What's wrong with it?" — model presentation; the user finds errors faster than they generate content
- "How would you explain this to someone who knows nothing about it?" — articulation test; if you can't explain it simply, you may not understand it
- "How would you apply this tomorrow? What would you do differently?" — application test; genuine understanding changes behavior
- "What would change if [key context variable] were different?" — robustness probe; does the understanding generalize or is it context-bound?
- "What's the strongest objection to this understanding?" — adversarial testing; genuine understanding can name its own weaknesses
- "What's still uncertain? What don't we know?" — uncertainty identification; knowing what you don't know is part of understanding
- "If you had to write a one-sentence version, what would it say?" — compression test; can the understanding be distilled?
- "Six months from now, what from this will you still remember? What matters most?" — salience test; separates the essential from the interesting
- "What would make you revisit this understanding?" — durability test; under what conditions should this be re-examined?
- "Who else would benefit from reading this? What would they get from it?" — audience test; understanding is relational
- "Does this feel true, or does it just feel tidy?" — authenticity check; neat frameworks can disguise shallow understanding

**Selection principle:** Verification questions should be challenging, not confirming. A verification phase that feels comfortable is failing. The user should have to think to answer. If every verification question gets an easy "yes," the questions aren't testing anything meaningful.

## Selection Principles

How to choose among candidate questions at any moment in the inquiry:

**Maximize uncertainty reduction.** Ask the question you're most uncertain about the answer to. If you already know what the user will say, the question is wasted — it confirms rather than informs. The ideal question is one where multiple plausible answers would lead you in different directions.

**Filter through appropriateness.** A question can be informationally optimal but socially wrong. Don't ask deeply personal questions in the first exchange. Don't ask basic factual questions after the user has demonstrated expertise — it communicates that you weren't listening. Don't ask three hard questions in a row without a moment of consolidation. A question that's right in content but wrong in timing or tone loses more than it gains.

**Match cognitive level to user state.** Don't ask evaluation questions when facts haven't been established. Don't ask for examples when the user is still trying to articulate the concept. Don't ask for synthesis when the components haven't been explored. The default progression — fact → explanation → evaluation → creation — is a guide, not a rule. But departing from it should be a conscious choice, not an accident.

**Cost awareness.** Every question costs patience. Questions that could be inferred from context are expensive — they spend goodwill on zero information. Questions that open genuinely new lines of thinking are investments — they generate engagement and material. Keep the balance positive. If you've asked several hard questions, follow with something lighter that lets the user demonstrate competence and build momentum.

**Anomaly priority.** Unexpected answers are more informative than expected ones. When an answer surprises you, follow up on the surprise immediately before returning to your planned sequence. The planned sequence was based on your prior model; the surprise means your model was wrong in an interesting way. The surprise is where the understanding lives.

**One question at a time in early phases.** In Phase 0-1, ask one question and let the user respond fully. Don't stack questions. The user's full, uninterrupted response to a single question is far more informative than fragmented responses to multiple simultaneous questions. In Phase 3, you can present 2-3 questions alongside your working model because the user now has enough context to handle multiple threads. Never ask more than 3 questions in a single turn — it fragments attention and produces shallow answers to all of them rather than deep answers to any.

**Silence is a question.** Sometimes the most productive move is to present your current model and say nothing else. Let the user react. Their unprompted response — what they correct, what they add, what they ignore — is often more revealing than their answer to any specific question you could ask.
